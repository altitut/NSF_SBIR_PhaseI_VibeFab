\subsection*{Overview}
This project proposes \VibeFab, an evidence-bounded, multimodal intent-to-build system that helps community-college learners, makers, artists, and first-time hardware founders translate ambiguous creative ideas into safe, buildable, and testable low-voltage physical-computing projects. These users often describe desired experiences rather than engineering specifications and may omit constraints involving power, heat, interfaces, firmware, fabrication, user contact, available tools, and testing. \VibeFab\ will combine natural-language intent, optional sketches/images, manufacturer documentation, validated reference designs, and physical measurements in a traceable workflow. It will identify missing requirements, ask high-value clarification questions, adapt only reference-design elements that remain within machine-checkable validity boundaries, and recommend tests that reduce decision-critical uncertainty. Outputs will distinguish supported facts, inferred assumptions, test-required claims, and unsupported conditions; the system will abstain or escalate when evidence or safety support is insufficient. Phase I will deliver a research prototype validated on four low-voltage project families and three physical prototypes.

\textbf{Keywords:} human--AI interaction; multimodal learning; engineering design education; uncertainty-aware AI; constraint reasoning; embedded systems; physical computing; selective prediction; active testing; hardware safety; intelligent tutoring; beginner electronics.

\textbf{Subtopic:} LC4. Innovative Approaches to Multimodal Learning.

\subsection*{Intellectual Merit}
This Small Business Innovation Research Phase I project will investigate whether ambiguous, multimodal creative intent can be converted into decision-ready engineering constraints and safely mapped to validated low-voltage reference designs without unsupported assumptions. The central technical hurdle is determining what is missing, which evidence supports each design claim, whether a customization remains inside a reference design's validity boundary, which physical test most efficiently resolves uncertainty, and when the system should stop. Phase I will develop three linked methods: (1) a multimodal evidence-contract graph and uncertainty-aware clarification policy; (2) machine-checkable bounded reference adaptation with counterexample-guided diagnosis; and (3) decision-guided physical testing that updates the evidence model and produces skill-adaptive guidance with calibrated abstention. The methods will be evaluated on an expert-annotated benchmark and three instrumented prototypes against LLM-only, retrieval-augmented, static-template, fixed-test, and ablated workflows. Metrics include critical-requirement recall, evidence coverage, adaptation accuracy, safety-conflict recall, abstention recall, test efficiency, expert correction time, and physical redesign outcomes.

\subsection*{Broader Impacts}
\VibeFab\ could broaden participation in physical creation by helping learners and creators enter the design--build--test cycle with clearer requirements, transparent evidence, safer boundaries, and better preparation for expert feedback. Community and technical colleges and makerspaces could support more varied projects while reducing avoidable failed builds, material waste, and one-to-one troubleshooting. The project advances responsible AI practice by making uncertainty visible, limiting automation to reviewed low-voltage domains, and preserving human engineering review for higher-risk decisions. Phase I will measure technical safety and usability; later pilots will track instructor workload, project completion, rework, learner understanding, and breadth of participation. The initial commercial product will be an annual institutional software license for physical-computing, creative-technology, makerspace, entrepreneurship, and introductory electronics programs. Successful Phase I results will provide the benchmark evidence, validated reference structures, safety controls, and pilot-readiness package required for Phase II institutional validation and scalable commercialization.

\subsubsection*{1.1 Technical Problem, Customer Context, and Phase I Scope}

A non-expert beginner may describe a project as ``a lightweight hat that reacts to music'' or ``a portable camera that recognizes visitors'' while missing battery runtime, current draw, temperature limits, sensor range, latency, enclosure ventilation, available tools, and required tests. Instructors can identify many of these omissions, but supporting multiple open-ended projects creates repeated one-to-one troubleshooting. AI-assisted coding has reduced novice effort in software, while also introducing risks of overreliance and unsupported output \cite{liffiton2023codehelp,kazemitabaar2023aicode,kasneci2023chatgpt,yan2024practical,crompton2023highered,southworth2023aicurriculum}. Physical creation is more tightly constrained because incomplete requirements interact with component interfaces, power and thermal limits, firmware, fabrication, materials, human contact, and measurement.

Existing tools most directly relevant to this problem fall into \textbf{three broad categories}. \textbf{General-purpose large language models (LLMs)} can generate parts lists and instructions, but fluent output alone does not establish compatibility, safety, or testability. LLMs may introduce unsupported assumptions, miss relevant evidence in long contexts, or produce internally coherent but weakly grounded claims \cite{ji2023hallucination,farquhar2024semanticentropy,manakul2023selfcheckgpt,liu2024lostmiddle,huang2024selfcorrect,nist2024genaiprofile}. \textbf{Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)} improves access to technical documentation, but current RAG methods are not specifically designed to identify missing engineering requirements or determine whether a proposed customization remains within a reference design's validated operating region \cite{asai2024selfrag}. \textbf{Professional electronic design automation (EDA)} tools provide powerful synthesis, constraint-checking, and verification capabilities, but are designed primarily for users who can express formal requirements and interpret technical outputs. Recent AI-for-EDA research has focused largely on circuit and register-transfer-level synthesis, tool orchestration, and domain-specific assistance, leaving an open research gap in translating non-expert creative intent into bounded, evidence-supported build decisions \cite{liu2023chipnemo,vijayaraghavan2024circuitsynth,zhang2024analogxpert,thakur2024verigen,lu2024rtllm,he2024chateda,chen2026ai4eda}.

The resulting challenge is a decision problem under incomplete, multimodal evidence (Figure~\ref{fig:creator_to_build_workflow}). Before producing a build plan, a creator-support system must identify missing or conflicting requirements, trace each engineering claim to its source, determine whether customization remains within validated electrical and physical boundaries, select the most informative feasible tests, and recognize when clarification or qualified engineering review is appropriate.

To address this gap, this project proposes an \textbf{evidence-bounded multimodal intent-to-build reasoning layer} that transforms incomplete creative intent into traceable requirements, bounded adaptation decisions, and measurement-informed test plans (Section 1.2). 
%Section~1.2 presents the three linked technical innovations underlying this architecture.
Phase I will develop and evaluate the method within a controlled low-voltage physical-computing scope. The research will cover USB- or battery-powered projects developed within reviewed educational and maker-oriented safety limits. Four benchmark families will represent distinct forms of creative hardware development: an IoT smart-camera platform, sound-reactive wearable lighting, an environmental sensing display, and an interactive tabletop installation. Three systems will be physically constructed and instrumented to evaluate missing-requirement identification, bounded reference adaptation, physical testing, and measurement-informed redesign. This scope provides the controlled variation needed to demonstrate technical feasibility and prepare the methods for expansion to additional hardware domains.

\subsubsection*{1.2 Proposed Technical Innovation and Product Architecture}

The proposed innovation is an \textbf{evidence-bounded multimodal intent-to-build reasoning layer} that translates creative intent into traceable, testable, and bounded engineering decisions. It integrates machine-checkable evidence representation, uncertainty-aware clarification, executable reference-design boundaries, counterexample-guided diagnosis, and measurement-informed physical testing. LLMs, retrieval, document parsers, manufacturer documentation, and EDA utilities serve as supporting components; the core R\&D is the reasoning and assurance layer that determines what evidence is sufficient, what requires clarification or testing, and when expert review is appropriate \cite{liang2023helm,nist2023airmf,nist2024genaiprofile,angelopoulos2023conformal,xiong2024uncertainty}.

Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} presents the three linked technical innovations developed in Phase I. Their continuity with the invited Project Pitch and Altitut's existing foundation is described in Section 1.3.

\textbf{Innovation 1: Multimodal evidence contract and clarification.}
User language, sketches, images, reference documents, and project context will be converted into typed requirements, claims, constraints, evidence sources, and tests. Each critical claim will retain provenance, units, validity conditions, uncertainty state, safety relevance, and verification status. A clarification policy will prioritize questions according to safety relevance, expected uncertainty reduction, downstream design impact, and user burden \cite{liu2023llava,li2023blip2,yue2024mmmu,blecher2024nougat,peng2025dochmet}.

\textbf{Innovation 2: Machine-checkable bounded adaptation and diagnosis.}
Reviewed reference designs will encode components, interfaces, dependencies, allowable ranges, known failure modes, and required tests. Candidate adaptations will be checked against executable invariants and validity boundaries, then classified as reuse, modify, test-required, expert-review-required, or outside the supported space. Boundary violations will produce structured counterexamples identifying the affected requirement, conflicting evidence, and feasible alternatives.

\textbf{Innovation 3: Decision-guided physical testing and skill-adaptive guidance.}
Physical tests will be ranked by their expected reduction of decision-critical uncertainty subject to time, cost, equipment, and safety constraints. Measurements will update the evidence model and refine the adaptation decision, while the validated plan will be rendered at different instructional levels without changing its evidence links, warnings, or stop conditions \cite{driess2023palme,brohan2023rt2,torne2024realtosim,madaan2023selfrefine,shinn2023reflexion,huang2024selfcorrect}.

The central scientific and engineering insight is that support for non-expert physical creation should be based on \emph{selective, evidence-bounded decision making}. VibeFab extends uncertainty-aware AI from language-level confidence to structured engineering claims, reference-design validity, and physical verification \cite{kuhn2023semanticuncertainty,farquhar2024semanticentropy,angelopoulos2023conformal}. Phase I will evaluate whether this architecture can improve engineering correctness, evidence coverage, test efficiency, and useful safe-case coverage across both benchmark cases and physical prototypes.

\subsubsection*{1.4 Technical Objectives and Research Methods}

\paragraph{(1) Objective 1: Multimodal Evidence-Contract Graph and Clarification Policy}~{}

\textbf{Research question.} Can \VibeFab\ identify critical and safety-relevant missing requirements from multimodal creative intent while asking substantially fewer questions than a static engineering questionnaire?

\textbf{Representation.} Each case will be represented as a typed graph $G=(V,E)$. The graph is intentionally more restrictive than free-form multimodal prompting because current vision-language and document models can extract useful content while still failing on expert-level diagrams, tables, and layout-dependent reasoning \cite{liu2023llava,li2023blip2,yue2024mmmu,blecher2024nougat,peng2025dochmet}. Node types will include user goals, functional requirements, physical constraints, safety constraints, components, interfaces, firmware dependencies, evidence sources, claims, tests, observations, and unresolved issues. Edges will represent support, conflict, dependency, derivation, modification, and verification. Each critical claim $c$ will store: source identifier; extracted value and unit; allowed interval or categorical state; applicability conditions; evidence strength; safety class; and verification state $z_c\in\{supported, inferred, test\mbox{-}required, unsupported\}$. Deterministic unit normalization and constraint checks will operate after model-based candidate extraction so that a fluent output cannot bypass machine-checkable rules.

\textbf{Uncertainty and clarification.} The method will maintain a decision-risk score rather than treating model confidence as factual correctness, because black-box confidence elicitation remains imperfect and can be systematically overconfident \cite{xiong2024uncertainty}. For candidate clarification question $q$, the initial ranking function $S$ will be
\[
S(q)=w_sR_s(q)+w_u\Delta U(q)+w_dD(q)-w_bB(q),
\]
where $R_s$ is safety relevance, $\Delta U$ is estimated reduction in unresolved decision risk, $D$ is downstream design impact, and $B$ is user burden. The weights will be estimated on the development set and then frozen. The project will compare this policy with a static questionnaire, a safety-first heuristic, and an LLM-selected question. A question is useful only if it improves requirement completeness or changes a downstream adaptation/test decision; conversational plausibility alone will not count as success.

\textbf{Multimodal evidence.} Text prompts will be supplemented in selected cases by sketches or reference images, datasheet tables/figures, and observations from prior build attempts. Document/image parsers will propose candidate facts; the evidence graph will retain the source region and require unit and applicability checks. An ablation will test whether multimodal evidence improves requirement and interface extraction relative to text-only input without increasing unsupported claims.

\textbf{Objective 1 output.} The output is an expert-reviewable requirement contract: explicit and inferred constraints, unresolved items, ranked clarification questions, provenance links, and a risk state that determines whether reference adaptation may proceed.

\paragraph{(2) Objective 2: Machine-Checkable Reference Adaptation and Counterexample-Guided Diagnosis}~{} % Bounded Reference replaced by "Reference" only

\textbf{Research question.} Can a system adapt validated reference designs to diverse creative goals without reusing modules outside supported electrical, physical, firmware, fabrication, or human-contact boundaries?

\textbf{Reference schema.} Following reviewed reference families will encode: component and module identifiers; interfaces and pin mappings; equations and unit constraints; nominal and allowable ranges; firmware/library dependencies; physical assumptions; fabrication constraints; known failure modes; expected observations; required tests; and escalation rules. 
The initial library will 
% not be a generic component catalog. It will 
be a bounded set of validated design structures that make the adaptation decision experimentally testable. This directly addresses recent hardware-generation benchmarks, which demonstrate that syntactic plausibility does not equate to functional or design-quality correctness \cite{liu2023verilogeval,thakur2024verigen,lu2024rtllm}.

\textbf{Adaptation procedure.} Given the requirement contract from Objective 1, the system will generate candidate module substitutions or parameter changes. A constraint engine will then evaluate hard invariants (for example, voltage compatibility, current budget, interface protocol, required level shifting, thermal/user-contact limits, and firmware dependency compatibility) and soft limits that require physical testing. Each proposed change will receive one of five labels: reuse, modify-within-boundary, test-required, expert-review-required, or outside-supported-space. The adaptation record will state the changed requirement, affected modules, supporting evidence, violated or preserved invariant, and required verification.

\textbf{Counterexample-guided diagnosis.} When a candidate violates a boundary or when a physical observation contradicts the expected range, the system will construct a counterexample record. 
% rather than simply regenerate an answer. 
The record will identify competing explanations, such as component mismatch, parameter error, model-form limitation, environment, measurement error, fabrication variation, or unsupported use, and propose the smallest set of constraint changes or tests that could distinguish them. This preserves continuity with the invited Pitch's assurance and real-to-sim-to-real concepts while keeping Phase I within low-voltage, reviewable models.

\textbf{Ablation and transfer.} The full method will be compared with (a) LLM-only adaptation, (b) RAG using the same documents, (c) rules without the evidence graph, and (d) the full method without counterexample generation. Transfer will be tested by holding out component substitutions and creative customizations within each reference family. Success requires generalization beyond memorized examples, not only correct reproduction of the development references.

\textbf{Objective 2 output.} The output is a machine-checkable adaptation contract that specifies what can be built now, what must be tested first, what requires expert review, and why.

\paragraph{(3) Objective 3: Decision-Guided Physical Testing, Model Update, and Skill-Adaptive Guidance}~{}

\textbf{Research question.}
% Can a limited set of safe physical tests efficiently resolve decision-critical uncertainty, reduce expert correction and redesign effort, and produce guidance that beginners can follow while preserving engineering limits?
Can a set of safe physical tests resolve decision-critical uncertainty, reduce correction and redesign effort, and produce guidance beginners can follow while preserving engineering limits?

\textbf{Test policy.}
Motivated by embodied multimodal and real-to-sim-to-real systems that update decisions from physical observations \cite{driess2023palme,brohan2023rt2,torne2024realtosim}, the system will rank each feasible test \(t\) by its expected reduction in unresolved decision risk relative to practical burden:
\[
J(t)=
\frac{\mathbb{E}[\Delta R(t)]}
{\alpha C(t)+\beta T(t)+\gamma H(t)+\epsilon}.
\]
Here, \(\mathbb{E}[\Delta R(t)]\) is the expected decision-risk reduction; \(C(t)\) is monetary and material cost; \(T(t)\) is setup and execution time; and \(H(t)\) captures equipment availability, component stress, and hardware risk. The weights place these burdens on a common scale, and \(\epsilon>0\) stabilizes near-zero-burden scores. Higher \(J(t)\) indicates greater expected risk reduction per unit burden. Unsafe or infeasible tests will be excluded before ranking.

Candidate tests will include current draw, voltage rails, temperature, communication latency, sensor response, packet loss, and controlled component substitution. The learned policy will be compared with a fixed expert checklist and random feasible-test ordering to determine whether selected tests produce design-relevant evidence more efficiently (Figure~\ref{fig:objective3_loop}).

\textbf{Physical validation.} Three prototypes will be constructed: an IoT smart-camera system, a sound-reactive wearable-lighting system, and an environmental sensing/display system. Controlled perturbations will include power variation, sensor bias/noise, latency or packet loss, component substitution, enclosure/thermal variation, and firmware mismatch. Measurements will update the evidence graph until the plan is supported within its bounded operating region, a safer alternative is selected, or qualified review is required.


\textbf{Skill-adaptive guidance.} Guidance will be rendered from the validated adaptation and test record rather than regenerated for each user. Beginner, maker/learner, and early-founder profiles will vary explanation depth, tool assumptions, sequencing, and troubleshooting while preserving the same evidence, warnings, unresolved issues, expected measurements, and stop conditions. Structured walkthroughs will evaluate participants' ability to identify and explain the next safe action.

\textbf{Selective prediction.} Risk--coverage curves will evaluate useful-guidance/appropriate-abstention balance. Thresholds selected on a calibration subset will be frozen before held-out evaluation. Required-abstention recall will measure unsafe or unsupported case detection, while safe-case coverage will measure retained guidance on expert-supported cases.

\subsubsection*{1.5 Key Technical Risks, Mitigation, and Fallback Approaches}

The principal Phase I risks arise when incomplete user intent, reference-design assumptions, and physical observations do not align. Each risk is tied to a concrete failure mode, a mitigation that will be tested during Phase I, and a narrower fallback that preserves a useful and supportable product capability. Quantitative success thresholds are defined separately in Section~1.6.

\paragraph{(1) Objective 1 risk: incomplete or inefficient intent recovery.}~{} 

Users may describe goals as ``portable,'' ``lightweight,'' or ``safe'' without measurable limits. Text, sketches, references, and context may be incomplete or inconsistent. Thus, system may miss a critical requirement, treat inferred values as confirmed, or ask questions irrelevant to downstream design/test decisions.
\textbf{Mitigation.} VibeFab will preserve unknowns and retain each constraint's source, units, applicability, uncertainty, safety relevance, and verification status. Clarification questions will be prioritized by decision-risk reduction, safety relevance, downstream impact, and burden. Unit, range, and cross-modal consistency checks will identify conflicts before adaptation.
\textbf{Fallback.} If the learned clarification policy offers no advantage, VibeFab will use family-specific, risk-weighted templates and require confirming safety-critical values. Persistently ambiguous families will be limited to guided project cards and approved formats.

\paragraph{(2) Objective 2 risk: hidden invalidation of a reference design.}~{}

A customization may preserve a reference design's function while invalidating an underlying assumption about power, heat dissipation, interface timing, firmware compatibility, enclosure geometry, environmental exposure, fabrication, or human contact. The most consequential failure is a plausible adaptation whose unsupported dependency stays hidden.
\textbf{Mitigation.} Each adaptation decision will be traced to invariants, typed dependencies, validity ranges, tests, and expert-review triggers. Controlled in-bound, boundary, and out-of-bound modifications will identify where assumptions do not propagate across modules. High-consequence power, thermal, interface, and user-contact constraints will receive deterministic checks and model-based reasoning.
\textbf{Fallback.} If adaptation cannot be maintained across the four benchmark families, VibeFab will support fewer validated reference families with narrower customization regions. Medium- and high-risk changes outside those regions require engineering review.

\paragraph{(3) Objective 3 risk: physical tests do not resolve the relevant uncertainty.}~{}

A ranked test may be easy yet unable to distinguish among explanations such as inadequate power, sensor noise, firmware mismatch, communication latency, or enclosure-related heating. Measurements may be noisy, incomplete, or require unavailable equipment. In these cases, the system could add testing effort without improving design decision.
\textbf{Mitigation.} Candidate tests will be filtered for safety, feasibility, tool availability, and minimum measurement quality. The evidence graph will record whether result eliminates a hypothesis, changes adaptation decisions, or reduces residual risk. Competing failure explanations will remain until evidence distinguishes them, and family-specific priors will be used where general ranking is unreliable.
\textbf{Fallback.} If test selection does not outperform simpler procedures, VibeFab will use family-specific decision trees or expert-authored sequences. Unresolved cases will present remaining hypotheses and require human approval before build guidance is issued.

\paragraph{(4) Integrated risk: workflow complexity exceeds demonstrated value.}~{}

The evidence graph, adaptation engine, test policy, and selective-prediction layer may improve traceability without reducing expert correction, redesign, or user effort enough to justify the workflow. Integration may introduce inconsistencies from stage-mismatched updates to evidence states and module decisions.
\textbf{Mitigation.} Component ablations and baselines will identify modules with value. Cross-module consistency checks will ensure adaptation decisions, test results, unresolved issues, and abstention states share one evidence record. Physical validation will prioritize three prototypes; the fourth family may remain benchmark-based if additional construction would dilute validation quality.
\textbf{Fallback.} Phase II pilots require the safety-critical gates in Section~1.6 and measurable operational advantage. If only one or two components demonstrate value, the product will narrow to the validated capability, such as reference-boundary checking, physical-test planning, or expert-review support.

\subsubsection*{1.7 Experimental Design and Evaluation Protocol}
\paragraph{(1) Benchmark construction}~{}

Following multi-scenario evaluation principles used in foundation-model, multimodal, and hardware-generation benchmarks \cite{liang2023helm,yue2024mmmu,liu2023verilogeval}, Phase I will construct 120 controlled cases: 30 cases in each of the four project families. Cases will be stratified into three levels: 40 routine inbound customization cases, 40 ambiguous or conflicting requirement cases, and 40 boundary/safety stress cases. Each family will include text-only cases and multimodal cases containing a sketch/image, datasheet excerpt, or build observation. Eighty cases will be used for method development and calibration; 40 cases will form a locked held-out test set. Near-duplicate variants and modifications of the same base configuration will remain in the same split to prevent leakage.

% Each case will be independently annotated by two qualified electrical/embedded-systems reviewers, including the Key Personnel and a budgeted independent engineering reviewer. 
Each case will be independently annotated by the PI and Key Personnel using a predefined codebook. The Key Personnel will approve safety-critical electrical decisions, and disagreements will be resolved before the benchmark is frozen.
The annotation protocol will define critical requirements, acceptable defaults, safety-relevant omissions, evidence sufficiency, reference-boundary labels, required tests, expected observations, and abstention decisions. Disagreements will be adjudicated before the benchmark is frozen. The codebook will be revised until inter-rater agreement reaches Cohen's $\kappa\geq0.75$ on categorical decisions; continuous values will use tolerance bands defined by the reference design and measurement uncertainty. The agreement criterion is a benchmark quality gate, not a system-performance claim.

\paragraph{(2) Baselines and controlled comparisons}~{}

All computational baselines will receive the same user input and, where applicable, the same document corpus. The principal baselines (Table~\ref{tab:principal}) are designed to separate retrieval, generation, structural checking, and uncertainty control; this is important because reflective RAG, self-checking, and iterative refinement can improve outputs without guaranteeing engineering validity or reliable intrinsic correction \cite{asai2024selfrag,manakul2023selfcheckgpt,madaan2023selfrefine,shinn2023reflexion,huang2024selfcorrect}.

For stochastic models, the team will use fixed model versions, prompts, retrieval settings, and generation budgets and will run each held-out case three times. The primary reported result will aggregate case-level outcomes across runs; failure analysis will retain individual outputs.

\paragraph{(3) Statistical analysis}~{}

Held-out test set will remain locked until methods and thresholds are frozen. Binary paired outcomes will be analyzed with McNemar tests and bootstrap confidence intervals; ordinal or continuous paired outcomes such as correction time and number of questions will use paired $t$ tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests depending on distributional diagnostics. The report will include 95\% confidence intervals and effect sizes.%, not only point estimates. 
Because Phase I is a feasibility study, success will be determined by pre-specified technical thresholds and practically meaningful improvements rather than by statistical significance alone. This multi-metric, locked-evaluation approach is consistent with recent foundation-model evaluation practice \cite{liang2023helm}.

\paragraph{(4) User walkthroughs}~{}

Twelve to eighteen non-expert participants representing community-college learners, makers, or first-time founders will complete structured walkthroughs using held-out supportable cases. %This is not an efficacy trial. 
Participants will identify the next safe action, locate the evidence or measurement that supports it, and recognize a stop/escalation condition. No participant will perform hazardous work; all physical activities will remain within reviewed low-voltage procedures under supervision. Results will inform usability and Phase II pilot design; prior computing-education studies support evaluating both task completion and whether AI guidance preserves learner understanding and self-regulation \cite{liffiton2023codehelp,kazemitabaar2023aicode,crompton2023highered}.

\subsubsection*{3.1 Societal Benefit and Beneficiaries}
Physical prototyping can develop creativity, systems thinking, engineering judgment, and workforce-relevant skills, yet entry remains difficult for people who cannot translate a creative goal into entire engineering requirements \cite{guthaus2023icworkforce,papagiannis2024makerspace,alvarez2024physicalcomputing}. VibeFab's intended societal benefit 
%is not replacement of engineering education or expert review. It 
is earlier and more transparent entry into the design--build--test cycle: clearer requirements, visible evidence, explicit uncertainty, safer boundaries, and better preparation for instructor or engineer feedback.

Initial beneficiaries are community- and technical-college learners in electronics, embedded systems, creative technology, entrepreneurship, and capstone courses; artists and makers adding sensing, lighting, display, or interaction to physical work; instructors and makerspace staff supporting diverse projects with limited time; and hardware founders creating early prototypes. During Phase I, the direct outcome will be technical feasibility evidence and validated safety boundaries. Later in Phase II, the company will test performance in institutional settings. In later deployment, the intended outcomes are fewer avoidable failed builds, reduced material rework, lower troubleshooting burden, and broader participation in hands-on creation.

\subsubsection*{3.2 Measurement and Tracking}
Phase I broader-impact indicators will be grounded in technical and usability measures rather than untested learning claims: critical-requirement detection, safety-conflict detection, unsupported assumptions, expert correction time, build/test correctness, appropriate abstention, and participant ability to identify the next safe action. Phase II pilots will add implementation measures: number and diversity of supported project types, project completion and abandonment, instructor-reported troubleshooting time, material rework, learner explanation of evidence and uncertainty, and access across learner backgrounds. Altitut will report results by project family and user profile to identify where the system helps and where it creates unequal burden or excessive abstention.

\subsubsection*{3.3 Unintended Consequences and Safeguards}
Potential unintended consequences include unsafe build attempts, overtrust in generated instructions, suppression of creative intent through excessive constraints, privacy exposure from user project data or images, unequal performance across language and experience levels, dependence on proprietary model services, and reduced appreciation for human expertise. Safeguards include (Figure~\ref{fig:gantt}):
\begin{itemize}
\item a bounded low-voltage scope and clear exclusion of voltage, regulated devices, and product certification;
\item evidence labels, uncertainty states, required measurements, stop conditions, and expert-review triggers;
\item risk--coverage evaluation so safety is not achieved by hidden or indiscriminate refusal;
\item preservation of the user's creative goal while constraining only unsupported implementation choices;
\item accessibility-conscious interfaces, alternative text/input modalities, and plain-language explanations;
\item data minimization, role-based access, secure storage, and retention controls for institutional deployments;
\item human approval for new reference families and for higher-risk adaptations.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{4.1 Product, Buyer, User, and Value Proposition}
VibeFab will be delivered as institutional software that complements, rather than replaces, EDA tools, maker hardware, and instructor expertise. The initial economic buyer is a department chair, workforce-program administrator, makerspace director, innovation-center leader, or faculty lead with responsibility for laboratory outcomes and instructional capacity. Primary users are learners, makers, artists, and first-time founders; instructors and technical staff are approvers and reviewers.

The customer problem is operational as well as educational. One instructor or makerspace staff member may support many teams using different sensors, boards, libraries, enclosures, and fabrication methods. Existing options are fragmented: free AI tools provide fast answers but limited assurance; kits and tutorials are reliable but fixed; manufacturer references are component-specific; professional EDA tools are powerful but assume engineering inputs; and human experts remain the integration layer. VibeFab's value proposition is measurable: help users arrive at expert review with more complete requirements, fewer unsupported assumptions, explicit tests, and a traceable adaptation record, thereby reducing correction time and avoidable redesign while preserving customized projects.

This is a need-to-have when institutions permit open-ended projects but lack sufficient expert capacity to review every decision before parts are ordered or prototypes are fabricated. It is a nice-to-have where courses use fixed kits with no customization. The initial beachhead therefore excludes tightly scripted kit courses and targets programs in which learner choice is central and instructor troubleshooting is a documented bottleneck.

\subsubsection*{4.2 Customer Validation and Market Size}
The team has completed 53 discovery conversations with educators, workforce stakeholders, and employers. Recurring themes were the difficulty of supporting diverse projects, the time required to translate ideas into technical plans, late discovery of component or fabrication problems, and concern about trusting unverified AI guidance. Austin Community College has provided institutional support for discovery and pilot planning. During Phase I, the company will conduct 20 additional structured interviews focused on buyer role, budget ownership, procurement, acceptable safety controls, integration conditions, and renewal criteria. At least eight interviews will be with two-year institutions, four with makerspace/innovation-center leaders, four with four-year creative-technology or entrepreneurship programs, and four with workforce/employer stakeholders.

The market model is bottom-up. The United States has approximately 1,000 community colleges, many with career and technical, engineering-technology, computer, manufacturing, entrepreneurship, or makerspace programs \cite{aacc2025fastfacts}. The broader market includes four-year institutions, libraries and community makerspaces, incubators, and workforce-training organizations. The initial serviceable market assumption is 750 institutions with at least one relevant program. At a working annual contract value of \$18,000, this represents approximately \$13.5 million in annual serviceable software revenue. The five-year obtainable-market objective is 60 institutional contracts at an average \$20,000 annual contract value, or \$1.2 million in annual recurring revenue. These are planning assumptions, not top-down claims; Phase I discovery will test the institution count, program fit, and price sensitivity.

\subsubsection*{4.3 Business Model and Revenue Path}
The initial model is annual institutional licensing with three working tiers: a course/makerspace pilot at \$5,000--\$8,000 per year; a department or campus license at \$15,000--\$25,000; and a multi-program license beginning near \$40,000 with administrative controls, integrations, onboarding, and expanded reference libraries. Pricing will be validated by presenting buyers with explicit alternatives and procurement scenarios rather than asking only general willingness-to-pay questions.

The commercialization sequence is: 
\textbf{Phase I:} establish technical feasibility; validate buyer, workflow, controls, and price hypotheses; prepare a pilot-readiness package.
%\textbf{Phase II:} deploy three to five institutional pilots; expand reference families; add instructor approval, analytics, accessibility, privacy, and integration features; measure renewal criteria.
\textbf{Initial launch:} convert successful pilots to annual contracts and sell through direct institutional relationships, workforce networks, conference partnerships, and existing Altitut relationships.
\textbf{Scale:} develop repeatable onboarding, partner-created reference libraries subject to Altitut validation, and tiered support; expand to early hardware-founder programs after institutional safety and evidence controls mature.

The technical milestones directly affect revenue readiness. Requirement and clarification performance determines whether onboarding can be lightweight. Boundary and abstention performance determines whether an institution can trust the product. Expert-time and redesign reductions determine whether a buyer can justify renewal. Reference-family transfer determines gross-margin scalability because the product cannot require bespoke engineering for every new project.

VibeFab does not seek to replace professional EDA, lifecycle, simulation, or manufacturer-reference systems. Those tools remain important downstream environments and evidence sources. The Phase I research will address a different decision layer: determining what the user has not specified, what remains valid from a reference design, what must be tested, and when the system should abstain or require qualified review.

The initial commercial wedge is low-voltage physical-computing instruction and early prototype development in community colleges, makerspaces, creative-technology programs, and entrepreneurship courses. In these settings, users often start with incomplete goals, while instructors must support many different projects with limited time. VibeFab's value is therefore not unrestricted hardware automation; it is a bounded pre-build workflow that improves requirement completeness, constrains adaptation to reviewed references, makes assumptions visible, and reduces repetitive troubleshooting.

VibeFab's sustainable advantage will depend on assets that are not easily reproduced through access to a general-purpose model alone: expert-annotated ambiguous-intent cases, machine-readable reference applicability boundaries, records linking proposed changes to preserved or invalidated evidence, calibrated clarification and abstention policies, physical measurement and failure data, and institutional implementation knowledge. Phase I customer discovery will further test which alternatives customers currently use, where those alternatives fall short, and what technical evidence is required for pilot adoption and renewal.

\subsubsection*{4.5 Safety, Regulatory, Ethical, and IP Strategy}
The first product will remain within reviewed low-voltage educational and maker-oriented applications. It will not certify a design for sale, authorize hazardous electrical work, or replace a licensed engineer. Product controls will include supported-project declarations, versioned reference records, audit logs, instructor approval settings, stop/escalation states, and clear separation between generated guidance and validated measurements. Institutional deployment will address accessibility, student-data privacy, cybersecurity, data retention, and vendor review. Any later expansion into regulated domains will require a separate regulatory and technical strategy.
